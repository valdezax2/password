Password Attacks 101

Cybersecurity Fundamentals for College Students


What are they?

Password attacks are attempts to guess or steal your passwords to gain unauthorized access to your accounts. Think of it like a thief trying to pick a lock to get into your house.

Why do they matter?

Your accounts hold a lot of personal informationâ€”your emails, financial details, and social media presence. When a cybercriminal gets your password, they can pretend to be you.

Types of Attacks

The Main Methods

There are a few common ways attackers try to get your password. We'll focus on these four:

  • Brute Force
  • Dictionary Attacks
  • Spraying
  • Rainbow Tables

Brute Force Attacks

How it Works

A brute force attack is like trying every single combination on a number lock. The attacker's computer systematically tries every possible character combination until it finds the right password.

The Catch

This method is incredibly slow and can be easily stopped by account lockout policies (e.g., "Too many failed attempts, try again in 5 minutes").

Activity: Simulate a Brute Force Attack

Try to "brute force" the password "cat".

Dictionary Attacks

How it Works

Instead of trying every possible combination, an attacker uses a list of common words and passwords from a "dictionary." This is a lot faster than a brute force attack.

Analogy

Imagine trying to guess someone's password. A brute force attack would be guessing "a, aa, ab, ac..." while a dictionary attack would be guessing "password, 123456, football, summer."

Activity: Simulate a Dictionary Attack

The password is a common word from the list: ["password", "123456", "qwert", "welcome", "football"]. Can you find it?

Password Spraying

How it Works

This is a super-efficient version of a dictionary attack. Instead of trying many passwords on one account, an attacker tries one common password (like "Password123!") across many different accounts.

Analogy

Think of it like spraying a hose across a field. You're not aiming for one specific spot; you're just hoping to hit something. This method helps attackers avoid getting accounts locked out.

Rainbow Tables

How it Works

This is a bit more technical. Websites don't store your password in plain text. They use a one-way function called a "hash" to turn your password into a jumbled-up string of letters and numbers. A rainbow table is a pre-calculated list of hashes for common passwords.

Analogy

Think of a hash as a unique fingerprint for your password. A rainbow table is a giant database of fingerprints and the passwords they belong to. An attacker can find your password's "fingerprint" in the table to get your password.

Activity: Simulate a Rainbow Table Lookup

Enter a simple password (e.g., 'password') to see its hash and how quickly it's found in a simulated rainbow table.

Defenses

How to Protect Yourself

  • Brute Force & Dictionary Attacks: Use long, complex passwords that mix uppercase and lowercase letters, numbers, and symbols. Don't use common words.
  • Rainbow Tables: Use a "salt" with your passwords. A salt is a random piece of data added to your password before it's hashed, making it unique and useless in a rainbow table.

Activity: The Salt Defense

See how a salt changes the hash of a password, making a rainbow table useless. Enter 'password' and a random salt to see its unique hash.
